Findings

Seven things the field's content tells us

Each finding ends with where it lands in our own strategy documents — pillar, growth lever or guideline.

Finding 01

The consumer air war is loud; the enterprise feed is nearly empty

Telstra's mascot campaign (27M+ TikTok views), Vodafone's Ali Wong comeback and Superloop's "Shout Out" all fight for consumers. Enterprise content is confined to Telstra's LinkedIn showcase pages or absent entirely. Nobody consistently tells Australia's infrastructure story in-feed.

Fits: Infrastructure Stories pillar (30% of LinkedIn) · 2030 "lead with network ownership"
Finding 02

"Trusted" is being contested — by three rivals at once

Optus's "We're On It" rebuild, Aussie Broadband's Roy Morgan "Most Trusted Telco" and Macquarie's NPS story all claim trust. The 2030 ambition — "most trusted digital infrastructure platform" — is the same word. Rivals assert it; the winning move is to prove it with evidence, repeatedly.

Fits: 2030 ambition · Guidelines: factual statements, no superiority claims
Finding 03

The sovereign-AI narrative war starts in September

Macquarie's IC3 Super West opens Sept 2026 with Treasurer-grade PR; Telstra InfraCo is filming intercity fibre routes; Optus's social push lands H2. Whoever is already publishing AI-era infrastructure content when that wave hits owns the frame.

Fits: Digital Infrastructure Investment lever · Industry Voice pillar
Finding 04

People-fronted beats brand-fronted — everywhere

Aussie Broadband's staff-and-founder-led feed earns the sector's best trust story. Vocus's own highest-engagement post ever is a person (Darin Bodilly's 20-year milestone), not a product. The evidence says scale employee voices, not brand statements.

Fits: People & Culture pillar · #OurPeopleAreTheDifference · 2030 culture imperative
Finding 05

Rivals lean on superlatives; our lane is verifiable fact

Superloop runs on an Ookla "fastest" claim; Macquarie on "world-leading NPS". Our guidelines prohibit unsubstantiated superiority claims — and we don't need them. "Second largest network owner in Australia", 50,000km of fibre, 400G coast-to-coast, Australia's first private network over Starlink: all factual, all ownable.

Fits: Guidelines legal sensitivities · 2030 market-position language
Finding 06

Formats win; hashtags don't

What's working in the field is platform-native format craft: TikTok trends (Telstra), campaign film (Vodafone), data-centre tours on YouTube (Macquarie), document carousels on LinkedIn. Our guidelines already call hashtags fading and mandate PDF carousels — the field confirms both.

Fits: Guidelines: carousel-as-PDF, captions, 4:5 · YouTube playlist architecture
Finding 07

Don't chase reactive speed — win the planned game

Vodafone's price-attack banter and Superloop's meme campaigns rely on same-day social turnaround. Our governance (Head of Comms approval on all posts, 24–48h standard, GM sign-off for executive and sensitive content) is built for quality, not speed. That's not a weakness to fix — it's a reason to compete on serialised, planned, proof-led content where consistency beats velocity. Reserve reactive social for moments we own (outage transparency, milestone days), briefed early via the Asana urgent flow.

Fits: Guidelines approvals & governance · "consistency is more important than volume"

Strategy fit

Findings mapped to the three pillars

For each pillar of the 2026–27 social strategy: what the field does, where the gap is, and the plays — all within existing guidelines.

1

Infrastructure Stories

Primary: LinkedIn · 30% of LinkedIn mix · supports "lead with network ownership"

What the field does

Telstra InfraCo films intercity routes; Macquarie serialises the IC3 build with government-grade PR. Everyone else's infrastructure content is thin or non-existent — the in-feed infrastructure story is unowned.

The gap

Vocus banked eight strategy-level milestones in 12 months (Google Australia Connect, 400G coast-to-coast, Telesat, Roy Hill on Horizon, Starlink private network, TPG Day 1, Vocus Mobile, Pacific Connect QLD) — most were announced once, not serialised.

Plays

  • Proof-point engine: one milestone retold well each fortnight as a LinkedIn PDF carousel — build, partner, outcome, why it matters.
  • Build diary: the Sydney–Melbourne intercapital build as a recurring series, anchor-tenant era — the exact asset Telstra will film; get there first.
  • #DidYouKnow ties each fact (50,000km, 15,000km subsea, 20,000 buildings) to a customer outcome, per the "show value" rule.
2

People and Culture

Primary: Instagram + LinkedIn · IG mix 50/30/20 · supports 2030 "ambitious and disruptive culture" imperative

What the field does

Aussie Broadband is the sector's best people storyteller: staff-fronted, founder-visible, authentic to the point of naming support agents. It converts culture content into the market's strongest trust position.

The gap

Instagram ran ~7 posts in June against a 3–4/week strategy target, and our best-ever engagement (a 20-year people milestone) proves the demand we're under-serving. The grid, cadence and series exist on paper — the gap is execution volume.

Plays

  • #TakeFive weekly, not occasional — the flagship series at flagship cadence, shot per the event checklist (eye contact encouraged for social).
  • Milestone-people crossovers: the people building the intercapital route, field techs on Pacific Connect — People pillar carrying Infrastructure proof.
  • Employee advocacy loop: brief leaders' personal posts alongside brand posts; people-fronted content is the proven multiplier.
3

Industry Voice

Primary: LinkedIn + YouTube · 25% of LinkedIn mix · supports Fed Govt / Hyperscaler / Carrier segments

What the field does

Macquarie's executives own sovereign-AI commentary and stood the NSW Treasurer at a topping-out. Telstra buys thought-leadership presence at scale. Optus is rebuilding credibility through its CEO's voice.

The gap

The YouTube channel — our strategy's designated "storytelling hub", with six playlists already architected in the guidelines — is still in setup. Executive perspectives run below the 2–4/month strategy rhythm.

Plays

  • Launch YouTube against the existing playlist architecture — "How we connect", "Industry insights", "Vocus products" give the first three films their homes.
  • Two named executive voices/month on AI-era connectivity — GM-approval lead times planned into the calendar, not fought against.
  • Quarterly data franchise: "State of Australian connectivity" from our own network data — quotable, Chatham-House-safe, unmatchable by resellers.

Guardrails

Every play, inside the guidelines

The rules that shape execution — nothing above requires an exception.

Claims & tone

  • Factual, never superlative: "second largest network owner", "50,000km of fibre" — no "best/fastest/most" without substantiation, no competitor disparagement.
  • Story over sales on LinkedIn; hook → context → why it matters → CTA; never open with "At Vocus" or "We're excited to announce".
  • Chatham House discipline on event and government content — themes, not names.
  • Hashtags sparing: 2–3 LinkedIn, 1–3 Instagram, from the approved set.

Formats & craft

  • LinkedIn carousels upload as PDF — the swipeable format that outperforms native multi-image.
  • Instagram is 4:5 portrait, approved Canva templates only, grid consistency audited.
  • Captions mandatory on all video — ClipChamp, Moderat/Arial white with shadow, one-word bounce; SRT files for YouTube.
  • Posting windows: LinkedIn Tue–Thu 8am–2pm; Instagram 6–9am / 7–9pm.

Governance & workflow

  • Everything briefed in Asana Comms Tracker; posted links filed back to the task.
  • Head of Comms approves all posts (24–48h); GM approval (1–2 days) planned into every executive and sensitive item — the September series is scheduled around this, not against it.
  • Urgent flow reserved for genuine moments (milestones, incident transparency) — not competitor banter.

What we deliberately don't do

  • No consumer air-war spend-chasing — Telstra and Vodafone own that game and its budgets.
  • No claims-led stunts (Ookla-style superlatives) — substantiation risk our guidelines exclude.
  • No reactive attack content — approval SLAs make it slow, and the legal-sensitivity rules make it risky; planned proof beats fast banter.
